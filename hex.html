Tiling

Only three regular polygons can tile: triangles, squares, and hexagons.

Square Tiles

Squares tile by dividing themselves into smaller squares. Quite boring.

Triangle Tiles

Triangles tile by... Well they form hexagons.

Perimeter-to-Area Ratio

Out of the three tile-able regular shapes, the hexagon has the smallest perimeter-to-area ratio. This means it uses the minimal amount of lines possible to tile an area.

Strength

When hexagons meet, they create three 120-degree joints. This makes them one of the strongest tile structure.

Push/Pull Resistance

The three joint segments act together to form a resistance. Push on one end, the other two push back equally. Pull and they pull back.

Lightweight

Because hexagons can cover a large area with minimal lines, hexagonal grids are extremely lightweight. This combined with their strength means they’re ideal for aviation.

Honeycomb

Wax is expensive but honey must be stored. To solve this bees take advantage of the hexagon, minimizing wax usage and maximizing honey storage.

Vision

Retinas are cells at the back of our eyes that capture light, letting us see. These cells also take advantage of a hexagonal grid to let in as much light as possible with the least amount of cell walls getting in the way.

Snowflakes

When snowflakes begin forming by a small number of water molecules combining into a hexagon. As more molecules join, the structure continues building hexagons in a fractal pattern.

Saturn’s Hexagon

Hexagons even appear outside of Earth’s nature. At Saturn’s north pole, there is a hexagon-shaped cloud larger than twice of Earth’s diameter. The color changes gradually over several years and nobody can agree on why it exists.
